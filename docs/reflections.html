Weekly Reflections

Week 3 (2/3/26)

AI and originality

I think AI and the current state of LLMs is not original. Originality is driven by the subjective human experience, our goals, our struggles, our survival. AI is simply learning patterns from a corpera of data and predicting the next best thing. While different prompts may provide different outputs it is still more of a derivative work rather than original work.

What is AGI? How does AGI affect scientific research?

AGI is the idea that we can make AI systems broadly smart enough, that they can exist autonomously with the level of human intelligence or possibly even beyond that. AGI, if it were to come to fruition, would drastically change our rate of scientific discovery. It could streamline the entire discovery pipeline of literature review, finding gaps, hypothesizing novel ideas, etc.

Week 6 (2/24/26)

Start your first ML pipeline. What did the error analysis reveal that the accuracy score alone did not? How does overfitting relate to knowledge vs. noise

A error analysis is looking at what the model was getting wrong rather than just looking at the general accuracy score. Accuracy can make the model seem good overall, but the errors show where it is confused and what patterns it is consistently not understanding. Overfitting relates to knowledge vs noise because when a model overfits it is not really learning the deeper pattern. It is learning random details or quirks in the training data. Knowledge is what defines the generalization while noise is the part that only seems useful because it was in the training set.

Week 7 (3/3/26)

Prediction vs. explanation: when does it matter why something happens, and when is it enough to know what will happen? Give a policy or research example.

Prediction matters when we mainly care about what will happen next. For example, predicting which students might fail a class is useful because then teachers can intervene earlier. Explanation matters when we need to know why something is happening. If a school wants to make a real policy change, it needs to know if the issue is attendance, resources, prior knowledge, etc. Prediction tells us where the problem is while explanation tells us what to change.

Draw a simple causal diagram for a relationship in your research area. What confounders exist? How would you distinguish a causal claim from a predictive association?

In my research area of GISC, a simple relationship could be green space and urban heat.

green space -> urban heat

Some confounders could be income, population density, building density, and distance from downtown. These could affect both how much green space an area has and how hot it gets. A predictive association would just say areas with less green space are also hotter. A causal claim would say that less green space causes higher urban heat. To distinguish them, we would need to control for those outside factors or compare similar areas where the main difference is green space.

Week 8 (3/10/26)

How can text mining reveal patterns that close reading alone cannot? What are its blind spots?

Text mining can show patterns across a lot of writing that a person might not notice from reading one by one. It can show repeated words, common topics, sentiment, or how language changes over time. Close reading is still better for deeper meaning and context. The blind spot is that text mining can miss sarcasm, tone, and the reason something is being said. It can show that a pattern exists but not always what it actually means.

How NLP and LLM can assist in your research and provide data/assistance for your project? Give specific examples.

NLP and LLMs can help GIS research because a lot of spatial problems also have text connected to them. For example, city planning documents, public comments, news articles, or social media posts can all mention places and problems in those places. NLP could help pull out locations or group comments by topic. LLMs could help summarize long reports or help with code for cleaning data. For my project, this could help connect what people are saying about an area to what is happening spatially.

Name limitations and suggest solutions

Some limitations are bias, hallucinations, missing context, and incomplete data. If the data only represents certain people, the results will also be limited. LLMs can also make things up or summarize something wrong. A solution is to check the original sources, compare with other data, and not treat the model as the final answer. In GIS, it is also important to make sure the locations and spatial data are actually correct.

Write an AI for research guide (you can use bullet points) incorporating ethical, technical and academic concerns that could lead to more problems than solutions (e.g. Hinton).

  • Use AI as a helper, not as the actual researcher.
  • Check what it says because it can make up sources, facts, and methods.
  • Be aware of bias because biased data will lead to biased output.
  • Do not put private or sensitive data into AI tools without permission.
  • Be honest about when AI was used.
  • Check code and methods instead of assuming they work.
  • AI can create more problems than solutions if you trust it without understanding it.

Week 9 (3/24/26)

What can LLMs do well, and where do they fail? Evaluate one specific failure mode (hallucination, bias, reasoning error) you have encountered.

LLMs are good at summarizing, explaining concepts, writing code, and helping organize ideas. They are useful when I need a starting point or want something explained in a simpler way. They fail when they sound confident about something that is wrong. One failure mode I have seen is hallucination. For example, an LLM might give a source, method, or code function that sounds real but does not actually exist. This is a problem in research because it can make fake information look credible if you do not check it.

How might generative AI reshape your discipline in the next five years? What research tasks will change most, and what should remain human-driven?

In my discipline of GIS, generative AI will probably make data cleaning, coding, and report writing faster. It could help write Python or R scripts, automate ArcGIS or other common GIS workflows. The tasks that will change most are the repetitive parts of research, like formatting data, writing boilerplate code, and summarizing large amounts of information. The human part should still be deciding the research question, checking if the spatial analysis makes sense, understanding local context, etc.

Week 10 (3/31/26)

What is Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) and why does it matter for research? How does grounding generation in retrieved documents change the trustworthiness of AI output?

RAG is when an AI model retrieves outside documents first and then uses those documents to answer a question. This matters for research because the model is not only relying on what it learned during training. It can use actual sources, papers, reports, or data that are relevant to the question. Grounding the answer in retrieved documents makes it more trustworthy because the answer can be checked against the source. It does not make the AI automatically correct, but it gives the researcher something to verify.

What are the recent criticisms about RAG? Any suggestions for improving generation?

One criticism of RAG is that it can still give bad answers if it retrieves the wrong documents. It can also take pieces of text out of context or act like the retrieved source supports something when it does not. Another issue is that the model may still hallucinate even when documents are provided. To improve generation, the system should retrieve better sources, show citations, separate what comes from the source from what the model is adding, and admit when the documents do not answer the question.

Week 11 (4/7/26)

What is a knowledge graph and how does it differ from a traditional database or a vector store? Why are relationships between entities valuable for knowledge mining?

A knowledge graph is a way to store information as entities and relationships between them. A traditional database stores information in tables, and a vector store stores information based on similarity. A knowledge graph is different because it focuses on how things are connected. The relationships are valuable because they make you think about the connections, not just the individual entities. This is useful for knowledge mining because sometimes the important information is in the relationship between things.

Identify three entities and three relationships from your research domain that could form a small knowledge graph. How would this structure help answer questions that a flat dataset cannot?

In GIS, three entities could be neighborhood, green space, and urban heat. Three relationships could be neighborhood contains green space, green space reduces urban heat, and neighborhood experiences urban heat. This would help answer questions about how places, features, and outcomes are connected. A flat dataset could show values in rows and columns, but a knowledge graph makes the relationships easier to follow and query.

Week 12 (4/14/26)

What does “AI for Science” mean? How do agentic AI systems (planning, tool use, multi-step reasoning) change the research workflow compared to single-prompt interactions?

AI for Science means using AI to help with the scientific research process. It can help with like literature review, finding patterns in data, writing code, testing ideas, and explaining results. Agentic AI systems change the workflow because they can plan steps, use tools, and work through a problem over multiple steps. This is different from a single prompt where the model just gives you a single response. In research, this could make the process significantly faster, but the researcher still needs to check the work and decide if the results its spewing out actually make sense.
